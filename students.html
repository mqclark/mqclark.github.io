Students + Teaching

Learning science by doing science.

Students belong in the work of asking questions, testing ideas, interpreting evidence, and deciding what should come next.

A research-rich classroom

Start with a question worth answering.

In BIOL 202, a first-year course-based undergraduate research experience, students conduct authentic Drosophila optogenetics research on descending neurons. The course combines proposal-driven experimental design, shared protocols, peer feedback, and the productive uncertainty that accompanies real results.

The goal is not to simulate research. It is to make its habits visible and available.

What students practice

From curiosity to contribution.

01

Designing experiments

Turn a broad observation into a testable question, a feasible plan, and a clear prediction.

02

Working with uncertainty

Interpret imperfect data, identify confounds, and learn what a result does and does not support.

03

Seeing across scales

Connect molecular and cellular mechanisms to circuits, behavior, and the whole animal.

04

Sharing science

Explain a method, a figure, and an argument clearly enough that another person can build on it.

Mentorship

High expectations. Real support. Space to grow.

Good research training includes feedback that is direct, patient, and specific. Students are encouraged to develop technical confidence, intellectual independence, and the ability to collaborate generously.

Interested in the lab?

Prior research experience is not a prerequisite.

A thoughtful email describing what interests you, what you hope to learn, and which questions caught your attention is a strong beginning.
