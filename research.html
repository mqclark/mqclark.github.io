Research

Hunting the circuits behind movement.

The Clark Lab uses Drosophila to ask how neural circuits develop, link sensory information to motor output, and generate coordinated locomotor behavior.

Behavior is the outcome. Circuits are the question.

Movement is never just movement. It is the visible trace of nervous systems integrating signals, choosing among actions, and recruiting muscles with precise timing. Fly systems make those operations experimentally approachable, from identified neurons to whole-animal behavior.

01

Descending pathways

From brain signals to motor action

Descending neurons provide a route from higher-order processing to the circuits that organize movement. We study how these pathways contribute to larval locomotion and how their anatomy and developmental history can reveal functional logic.

02

Circuit development

How does a circuit become capable of behavior?

Neural identity, connectivity, and timing are built over development. We use genetics, anatomy, and behavior to ask how those pieces align into motor programs that work in an animal moving through the world.

03

Methods as bridges

Connecting genes, anatomy, and behavior

Our work draws on neurogenetic approaches, optogenetics, anatomical analysis, and behavioral measurement. The point is not a single technique; it is building a path from a specific cell type to an observable action.

“A circuit is not finished when it is wired. It is finished when it can move a body through the world.”
